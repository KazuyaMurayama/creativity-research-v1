## Category K: Emotion & Well-being (感情・ウェルビーイング)

---

### K-1: Positive Emotion Cultivation (ポジティブ感情の涵養)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Isen, Daubman & Nowicki (1987)**
- **Citation:** Isen, A. M., Daubman, K. A., & Nowicki, G. P. (1987). Positive affect facilitates creative problem solving. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 52(6), 1122-1131.
- **Design:** 4 experiments, laboratory-based. Positive affect induced via comedy film clips or small bag of candy.
- **Key Findings:** Positive affect group significantly outperformed neutral and negative affect groups on Duncker's candle task (~75% solved vs. ~20% in control) and the Remote Associates Test (RAT). Exercise-induced arousal (affectless arousal) did NOT produce comparable improvements, confirming the emotion-specific nature of the effect.
- **Effect Size:** Not reported as Cohen's d in original paper (pre-convention era). The candle task difference (~75% vs. ~20% solving rate) represents a very large effect.
- **Mechanism:** Positive affect broadens cognitive organization, facilitating perception of diverse characteristics and enabling novel combinations of elements.

**Paper 2: Baas, De Dreu & Nijstad (2008) — META-ANALYSIS**
- **Citation:** Baas, M., De Dreu, C. K. W., & Nijstad, B. A. (2008). A meta-analysis of 25 years of mood-creativity research: Hedonic tone, activation, or regulatory focus? *Psychological Bulletin*, 134(6), 779-806.
- **Design:** Meta-analysis synthesizing 102 effect sizes from 25 years of research.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Positive moods vs. neutral controls: **r = .15** (small but significant)
  - Negative moods vs. neutral: r = -.03 (not significant)
  - Critical moderator: **Activating positive moods** (happiness, excitement) with approach motivation produced the strongest creativity effects.
  - Deactivating positive moods (relaxation, serenity) did NOT reliably enhance creativity.
- **Effect Size:** r = .15 overall (converting to d ≈ 0.30, a small-to-medium effect). Activating positive moods likely produce larger effects.
- **Significance:** Resolves conflicting findings — it is not merely positive valence but the combination of positive valence + high activation + approach motivation that drives creativity.

**Paper 3: Fredrickson & Branigan (2005)**
- **Citation:** Fredrickson, B. L., & Branigan, C. (2005). Positive emotions broaden the scope of attention and thought-action repertoires. *Cognition and Emotion*, 19(3), 313-332.
- **Design:** 2 experiments, 104 college students. Film-elicited emotions (amusement, contentment, neutral, anger, anxiety). Global-local visual processing task + Twenty Statements Test.
- **Key Findings:** Positive emotions (vs. neutral) broadened scope of attention and thought-action repertoires. Negative emotions narrowed thought-action repertoires relative to neutral.
- **Mechanism:** Broaden-and-build theory — positive emotions expand the array of percepts, thoughts, and actions presently in mind.

**Paper 4: Ashby, Isen & Turken (1999)**
- **Citation:** Ashby, F. G., Isen, A. M., & Turken, U. (1999). A neuropsychological theory of positive affect and its influence on cognition. *Psychological Review*, 106(3), 529-550.
- **Design:** Theoretical/neuropsychological model with supporting evidence.
- **Key Finding:** Proposed that positive affect increases dopamine release in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and frontal cortical areas, improving cognitive flexibility and facilitating selection of cognitive perspective.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- Baas et al. (2008) show that **deactivating positive moods** (relaxed, serene) do NOT enhance creativity — the effect is specific to activating, approach-oriented positive states.
- Gable & Harmon-Jones (2010) found that certain negative emotions can also broaden attention, challenging the simplicity of the broaden-and-build framework.
- The meta-analytic effect size (r = .15, d ≈ 0.30) is smaller than some individual studies suggest — publication bias may inflate individual study results.

#### Mechanism Summary
Activating positive emotions (happiness, excitement) increase dopaminergic activity in frontal cortical areas, broadening attentional scope and enhancing cognitive flexibility. This facilitates the detection of remote associations and novel combinations of ideas. The effect is specific to high-activation, approach-motivated positive states.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **4** | Large meta-analysis (102 effect sizes) + multiple classic experiments. Consistent findings when accounting for activation level. Well-replicated core finding. |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **3** | Meta-analytic r = .15 (d ≈ 0.30) = medium. Individual studies (Isen) show larger effects, but meta-analysis provides more conservative estimate. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **5** | Watching a short comedy video or eating a piece of candy can induce the state. Requires no equipment, cost, or training. |

### K-2: Eudaimonic Well-being (ユーダイモニック・ウェルビーイング)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Conner, DeYoung & Silvia (2018)**
- **Citation:** Conner, T. S., DeYoung, C. G., & Silvia, P. J. (2018). Everyday creative activity as a path to flourishing. *Journal of Positive Psychology*, 13(2), 181-189.
- **Design:** 13-day daily diary study, n = 658 young adults. Measured daily creative activity, positive/negative affect, and flourishing.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Lagged multilevel models: People felt higher activated positive affect AND higher flourishing on days following days with more creative activity than usual.
  - Reverse direction (PA predicting next-day creativity) was NOT supported.
  - Effect: creativity → well-being (but not well-being → creativity in lagged analysis).
  - Replicated in a pandemic-era diary study (creative activity predicted positive emotions next day, β = 0.06).
- **Effect Size:** Small cross-day effects (coefficients in the 0.04-0.06 range in multilevel models). The relationship is reliable but modest in daily diary data.
- **Limitation:** Direction of causality is creativity → well-being, not the reverse. This does not directly show that cultivating eudaimonic well-being boosts creativity — rather, creative activity boosts eudaimonic well-being.

**Paper 2: Ryff & Singer (2008) — Conceptual Framework**
- **Citation:** Ryff, C. D. (2013). Psychological well-being revisited: Advances in the science and practice of eudaimonia. *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics*, 83(1), 10-28.
- **Design:** Theoretical/conceptual review.
- **Key Findings:** Identifies encounters with arts and nature as "forces for eudaimonia." Connects creativity and eudaimonic well-being conceptually but does not provide direct experimental evidence for a eudaimonia → creativity pathway.

**Paper 3: The Creative Life Study (2022)**
- **Citation:** Conner, T. S., et al. (2022). The Creative Life: A Daily Diary Study of Creativity, Affect, and Well-Being in Creative Individuals. *Creativity Research Journal*, 34(4).
- **Design:** Daily diary study of professional creative individuals.
- **Key Findings:** Confirmed bidirectional same-day link between creativity and well-being. Replicated the finding that creative activity predicts next-day well-being.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **The causal arrow is primarily creativity → well-being**, not the reverse. Direct RCT evidence showing that increasing eudaimonic well-being (purpose, meaning) causally enhances creative output is **scarce to nonexistent**.
- Most RCTs in the well-being literature (81.5% effective for strength enhancement per a 2025 synthesis) have used hedonic rather than eudaimonic outcome measures, and none have measured creative output as a dependent variable.
- The relationship may be best understood as a positive feedback loop rather than a one-directional causal pathway.
- Sternberg's investment theory of creativity emphasizes that creative work requires courage and persistence (which relate to purpose/meaning), but this is theoretical rather than experimentally validated.

#### Mechanism Summary
A positive feedback loop: creative activity produces eudaimonic well-being (flourishing, meaning, purpose), which in turn sustains intrinsic motivation for further creative engagement. Eudaimonic well-being provides the psychological resources (autonomy, competence, relatedness) needed for sustained creative effort. However, the direct causal evidence from eudaimonia to creativity is weaker than from creativity to eudaimonia.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **2** | Large diary studies show correlational/lagged associations, but NO RCTs testing the eudaimonia → creativity direction. Evidence is primarily diary-based and correlational. |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **2** | Small cross-day effects (β ≈ 0.04-0.06). The creativity → well-being direction is clearer, but the well-being → creativity path is not established with clear effect sizes. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **3** | Cultivating a sense of purpose/meaning requires sustained self-reflection and is not a quick fix. It involves identity-level change that takes ongoing effort. |

## Category L: Boredom & Daydreaming (退屈・空想)

---

### L-1: Intentional Boredom (意図的な退屈)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Mann & Cadman (2014)**
- **Citation:** Mann, S., & Cadman, R. (2014). Does being bored make us more creative? *Creativity Research Journal*, 26(2), 165-173.
- **Design:** Experimental. Participants performed a boring task (copying numbers from a phone directory for 15 minutes) before a creative task (Alternative Uses Task).
- **Key Findings:** Boredom condition produced significantly more ideas than control. More passive boredom (reading phone numbers) produced even higher creativity than active boredom (writing numbers), because passive boredom allows more daydreaming.
- **Effect Size:** Not reported as Cohen's d. Differences were statistically significant but specific effect magnitudes not detailed in accessible sources.

**Paper 2: Gasper & Middlewood (2014)**
- **Citation:** Gasper, K., & Middlewood, B. L. (2014). Approaching novel thoughts: Understanding why elation and boredom promote associative thought more than distress and relaxation. *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*, 52, 50-57.
- **Design:** 3 experiments with different mood inductions (boredom, elation, distress, relaxation) and measures of associative/novel thought.
- **Key Findings:** Bored and elated participants outperformed distressed and relaxed participants on measures of associative thought. The mechanism proposed: boredom (like elation) triggers an approach motivation that promotes novel thinking.
- **Significance:** Provides a theoretical framework — boredom's creative benefit comes from approach motivation, not from the boredom state per se.

**Paper 3: Haager, Kuhbandner & Pekrun (2016) — CONTRADICTORY**
- **Citation:** Haager, J. S., Kuhbandner, C., & Pekrun, R. (2016). To be bored or not to be bored — How task-related boredom influences creative performance.
- **Design:** Experimental study.
- **Key Finding:** Boredom **impaired** creative performance, directly contradicting Mann & Cadman (2014) and Gasper & Middlewood (2014).

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Results are highly inconsistent across studies.** Some find positive effects (Mann & Cadman 2014; Gasper & Middlewood 2014), others find negative effects (Haager et al. 2016), and others find no effect (Larson 1990).
- A 2024 scoping review (Zeissig et al.) in educational contexts concluded: "a clear causal, positive or negative effect of boredom on creativity is not currently supported by the empirical evidence available."
- Gasper & Middlewood (2014) measured boredom induction effectiveness only retrospectively (after the creativity task), which is a methodological weakness.
- Most studies used cross-sectional designs without repeated measures.
- The conclusion that boredom facilitates creativity has been described as "somewhat tenuous" based on the totality of evidence.

#### Mechanism Summary
The proposed mechanism: boredom signals that the current situation is unsatisfying, triggering approach motivation to seek novel stimulation. This promotes mind-wandering and activation of the default mode network (DMN), which supports associative thinking. However, this mechanism may only operate when boredom is mild/passive and when individuals have the disposition to respond with approach (rather than avoidance) motivation.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **2** | Few experimental studies with highly inconsistent results. Some find positive effects, others find null or negative effects. No meta-analysis. Methodological concerns (retrospective manipulation checks). |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **2** | When effects are found, they appear small to moderate, but inconsistency across studies makes it impossible to establish a reliable effect size. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **5** | Simply put away your phone and sit doing nothing for 15 minutes. No cost, no equipment, no training required. |

### L-2: Daydreaming / Mind-Wandering (デイドリーミング・マインドワンダリング)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Baird et al. (2012) — "Inspired by Distraction"**
- **Citation:** Baird, B., Smallwood, J., Mrazek, M. D., Kam, J. W. Y., Franklin, M. S., & Schooler, J. W. (2012). Inspired by distraction: Mind wandering facilitates creative incubation. *Psychological Science*, 23(10), 1117-1122.
- **Design:** Incubation paradigm. Participants did Unusual Uses Task (UUT), then were randomly assigned to: undemanding task (promotes mind-wandering), demanding task, rest, or no break, then repeated UUT.
- **Key Findings:** The undemanding task condition (highest mind-wandering) showed significant improvement on **repeated** UUT problems. No improvement on new problems. The improvement was associated with higher mind-wandering rates, not with directed thoughts about the task.
- **Effect Size:** **η² = .10** (medium effect) for the incubation condition on repeated-exposure problems.
- **Significance:** Highly influential study (~2,000+ citations). First experimental demonstration of mind-wandering's role in creative incubation.

**Paper 2: Smeekens & Kane (2016) — REPLICATION FAILURE**
- **Citation:** Smeekens, B. A., & Kane, M. J. (2016). Working memory capacity, mind wandering, and creative cognition: An individual-differences investigation. *Psychology of Aesthetics, Creativity, and the Arts*, 10, 389-415.
- **Design:** 3 individual-differences studies using incubation paradigm (one was an approximate replication of Baird et al. 2012).
- **Key Findings:** Failed to replicate the expected association between mind wandering and divergent thinking across all three experiments. In-the-moment task-unrelated thoughts did NOT correlate with divergent creativity.
- **Authors' conclusion:** "Especially problematic for claims that mind-wandering processes contribute to creative cognition."

**Paper 3: Murray et al. (2021) — DIRECT REPLICATION FAILURE**
- **Citation:** Murray, S., et al. (2021). Direct replication of Baird et al. (2012).
- **Design:** Direct replication attempt.
- **Key Findings:** Found an insignificant relationship between mind wandering and creative idea generation, further challenging the original finding.

**Paper 4: Rooij (2024)**
- **Citation:** Rooij, A. (2024). A wandering mind is not always a creative mind: How thought dynamics explain the relationship between mind wandering and creativity. *Journal of Creative Behavior*.
- **Key Finding:** The relationship between mind wandering and creativity depends on thought dynamics — not all mind wandering is equal. Only certain types of spontaneous thought may be beneficial.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Multiple replication failures** (Smeekens & Kane 2016, Murray et al. 2021, Steindorf et al. 2021, Smith et al. 2022) have failed to observe the reliable association between mind wandering and post-incubation creativity.
- The original Baird et al. (2012) finding may be more specific than initially thought: benefits only for repeated (not new) problems, and only in certain types of undemanding tasks.
- Recent reviews characterize the evidence as showing that "the circumstances under which positive associations between mind wandering and creativity can be found still need to be examined in more detail."

#### Mechanism Summary
The proposed mechanism: during undemanding tasks, the mind wanders and activates the default mode network (DMN), which facilitates unconscious associative processing of previously encountered problems (incubation). However, this mechanism appears fragile — it may only work for specific types of problems, specific types of mind-wandering (spontaneous rather than deliberate), and only when the person has already encoded the problem before the incubation period.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **2** | Original finding (Baird 2012) was influential but has faced multiple replication failures (Smeekens & Kane 2016, Murray 2021, Steindorf 2021). Evidence is currently inconsistent and contested. |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **2** | Original η² = .10 (medium), but given replication failures, the true effect may be much smaller or conditional on specific circumstances. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **5** | Simply engage in an undemanding task (e.g., walk, shower, light chores) and let the mind wander. No cost or preparation needed. |

## Category M: Divergent Thinking Training (思考トレーニング)

---

### M-1: Alternative Uses Task (AUT) Practice (代替用途課題の実践)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Scott, Leritz & Mumford (2004) — META-ANALYSIS**
- **Citation:** Scott, G., Leritz, L. E., & Mumford, M. D. (2004). The effectiveness of creativity training: A quantitative review. *Creativity Research Journal*, 16(4), 361-388.
- **Design:** Quantitative meta-analysis of 70 studies, 4,210 participants, yielding 97 effect size estimates.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Average effect size: **Δ = 0.64** (medium-to-large effect).
  - Effects held after controlling for internal validity.
  - More successful programs focused on **cognitive skills development** and **heuristics**, using **realistic exercises** appropriate to the domain.
  - Effects generalized across criteria, settings, and target populations.
- **Limitation:** Most studies were with children or university students, not organizational/professional contexts.

**Paper 2: McKay et al. (2024) — Organizational Meta-Analysis**
- **Citation:** McKay, A. S., Reiter-Palmon, R., Coombes, S. M. T., & Coombs, J. E. (2024). A meta-analysis of creativity training in organizational settings. *Creativity and Innovation Management*.
- **Design:** Meta-analysis of creativity training studies in organizational settings using Kirkpatrick's framework.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Overall training effect: **g = 0.68**
  - Learning outcomes: **g = 0.73** (significant)
  - Behavioral/transfer outcomes: **g = 0.34** (NOT significant)
  - Delayed assessment: g = 0.40 (significantly smaller than immediate g = 0.86)
- **Critical finding:** Training improves learning metrics but has **nonsignificant effects on actual on-the-job behavior/transfer.**

**Paper 3: APA 2024 Comprehensive Meta-Analysis**
- **Citation:** (2024). The impact of creativity training on creative performance: A meta-analytic review of 5 decades. APA.
- **Design:** Meta-analysis of 169 studies, 844 effect sizes across 5 decades.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Unadjusted effect: **0.53 SDs**
  - After publication bias adjustment: **0.29-0.32 SDs** (substantially lower)
- **Significance:** Demonstrates that publication bias significantly inflates creativity training effect sizes.

**Paper 4: Torrance Longitudinal Evidence (Validity)**
- **Citation:** Runco, M. A., Millar, G., Acar, S., & Cramond, B. (2010). Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking as predictors of personal and public achievement: A fifty-year follow-up. *Creativity Research Journal*.
- **Key Findings:** TTCT divergent thinking scores predicted real-world creative achievement across 9, 7, 22, 40, and 50-year follow-up studies — validating that DT measures tap into something meaningful for real creative output.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- The **transfer problem** is significant: McKay et al. (2024) found that behavioral/transfer outcomes of creativity training were nonsignificant (g = 0.34, ns).
- Publication bias inflates reported effect sizes substantially (from 0.53 to 0.29-0.32 after adjustment).
- Training improves performance on DT tasks but evidence for transfer to real-world creative ability is much weaker.
- Brain imaging studies show structural changes (prefrontal EEG alpha, white matter reorganization) from DT training, but the practical significance of these neural changes for everyday creativity is unclear.

#### Mechanism Summary
Regular practice with open-ended problems (like AUT) strengthens neural networks involved in flexible thinking, including frontal alpha synchronization and prefrontal connectivity. The practice habituates the cognitive stance of viewing problems from multiple perspectives. However, transfer to real-world creativity may require additional components: domain-specific knowledge, realistic exercises, and metacognitive heuristics.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **4** | Multiple meta-analyses (Scott et al. 2004, McKay 2024, APA 2024) with large sample sizes. Consistent finding that training improves DT task performance. However, transfer evidence is weaker. |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **3** | Meta-analytic Δ = 0.64 (Scott) and g = 0.68 (McKay) for learning, but drops to d = 0.29-0.32 after publication bias adjustment. Transfer effects are nonsignificant. Scoring conservatively based on adjusted estimates. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **5** | Can practice AUT (e.g., "list uses for a brick") at home today. Free, takes 5-10 minutes per session, no materials needed. |

### M-2: Creative Problem Solving (CPS) Frameworks (創造的問題解決フレームワーク)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Isaksen & Treffinger (2005) — Historical Review**
- **Citation:** Treffinger, D. J., & Isaksen, S. G. (2005). Creative Problem Solving: The history, development, and implications for gifted education and talent development. *Gifted Child Quarterly*, 49(4), 342-353.
- **Design:** Historical review of 50+ years of CPS research and development.
- **Key Findings:** CPS is one of the most widely used and researched creativity frameworks. "Hundreds of published studies" cited, though many are case studies and quasi-experimental designs.

**Paper 2: CPS Training with R&D Workers (Taiwan study)**
- **Citation:** (Quasi-experimental study with 106 R&D workers, time-series design)
- **Design:** 71 R&D workers received 12 hours of CPS training + 2 follow-up sessions over 1 year. 35 controls received no training. Pre/post Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking and Myers-Briggs Type Indicator. R&D performance tracked.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Fluency and flexibility scores increased significantly after CPS training.
  - Number of co-authored service projects increased significantly from pretest to posttest (no such change in controls).
- **Limitation:** Non-random assignment (volunteers vs. non-volunteers), small sample.

**Paper 3: Scott, Leritz & Mumford (2004) — Companion Content Analysis**
- **Citation:** Scott, G., Leritz, L. E., & Mumford, M. D. (2004). Types of creativity training: Approaches and their effectiveness. *Journal of Creative Behavior*, 38(3), 149-179.
- **Design:** Content analysis of 156 creativity training programs.
- **Key Findings:** Programs that combine CPS-style structured approaches (diverge-converge cycles) with realistic exercises and cognitive skill development are most effective. Delivery method matters as much as content.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **No true RCTs found** in the CPS literature specifically. Studies use quasi-experimental designs with non-random assignment and often self-selected volunteers.
- The "hundreds of published studies" claimed by CPS developers include many case studies and program evaluations, not rigorous experimental designs.
- Effect sizes from CPS-specific studies are rarely reported in standardized formats.
- Transfer to real-world organizational outcomes is supported by some evidence (R&D project output) but sample sizes are small.

#### Mechanism Summary
CPS provides a structured alternation between divergent thinking (idea generation) and convergent thinking (evaluation and selection), engaging both the default mode network and executive control networks. The framework provides metacognitive scaffolding that helps individuals navigate between creative exploration and practical implementation. The structural approach reduces "blank canvas" paralysis and provides heuristics for approaching novel problems.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **3** | Long history of research (50+ years) with consistent positive findings, but mostly quasi-experimental designs. No true RCTs. CPS-specific meta-analysis is lacking (though general creativity training meta-analyses include CPS-type programs). |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **3** | General creativity training meta-analyses show d = 0.64 (including CPS-type programs). The Taiwan R&D study shows practical significance (increased project output). But CPS-specific effect sizes are rarely isolated. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **3** | Requires learning the CPS framework (diverge-converge cycles, tools). Can be self-taught from books, but optimal use often involves facilitation training. Initial learning curve of several hours. |

## Category N: Music & Art (音楽・芸術的活動) — NEW CATEGORY

---

### N-1: Playing Instruments / Music Listening (楽器演奏・音楽聴取)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Ritter & Ferguson (2017) — Happy Music Study**
- **Citation:** Ritter, S. M., & Ferguson, S. (2017). Happy creativity: Listening to happy music facilitates divergent thinking. *PLoS ONE*, 12(9), e0182210.
- **Design:** 155 participants in 5 conditions (happy music, sad music, calm music, anxious music, silence). Measured divergent thinking (AUT) and convergent thinking (RAT, Idea Selection, Creative Insight).
- **Key Findings:**
  - Happy music (high arousal, positive valence — Vivaldi's "Spring") significantly enhanced overall divergent thinking (ODT) vs. silence: t(57) = 2.110, p = .039.
  - Happy music group: M = 93.87, SD = 32.02 vs. Silence: M = 76.10, SD = 32.62.
  - **No effect on convergent thinking** (RAT: F(4,150) = 0.36, p = .839, ηp² = .009).
  - Five-condition ANOVA did NOT survive Bonferroni correction (p values: .017-.039 uncorrected).
- **Effect Size:** Cohen's d ≈ 0.55 for the primary happy music vs. silence comparison on ODT (calculated from reported means and SDs). However, this did not survive correction for multiple comparisons.
- **Limitation:** Single study, modest sample per condition (~31), primary finding marginally significant and does not survive multiple comparison correction.

**Paper 2: Threadgold et al. (2019) — CONTRADICTORY**
- **Citation:** Threadgold, E., et al. (2019). Background music stints creativity: Evidence from compound remote associate tasks. *Applied Cognitive Psychology*, 33(5), 889-900.
- **Design:** 3 experiments testing background music (foreign lyrics, instrumental, familiar lyrics) vs. quiet conditions on Compound Remote Associates Task (CRAT).
- **Key Findings:** ALL three types of background music **significantly impaired** CRAT performance compared to quiet conditions. Library noise had no effect.
- **Authors' conclusion:** "Findings challenge the popular view that music enhances creativity, and instead demonstrate that music, regardless of the presence of semantic content, consistently disrupts creative performance in insight problem solving."
- **Important note:** This tested convergent/insight creativity (CRAT), not divergent thinking (AUT). The two studies may not contradict each other — music may help divergent thinking but harm convergent thinking.

**Paper 3: Palmiero et al. (2020) — Musical Training**
- **Citation:** Palmiero, M., et al. (2020). The relationships between musical expertise and divergent thinking. *Acta Psychologica*, 203, 103014.
- **Design:** Cross-sectional comparison of expert musicians, self-taught musicians, and non-musicians on musical, verbal, and visual divergent thinking.
- **Key Findings:** Expert musicians showed higher creative indices in musical AND verbal domains than self-taught musicians and non-musicians. No group difference in visual creativity.
- **Limitation:** Cross-sectional design — cannot determine causality (self-selection into music training).

**Paper 4: Sala & Gobet (2020) — SKEPTICAL META-ANALYSIS**
- **Citation:** Sala, G., & Gobet, F. (2020). Cognitive and academic benefits of music training with children: A multilevel meta-analysis. *Memory & Cognition*.
- **Design:** Meta-analysis (N = 6,984, k = 254, m = 54).
- **Key Findings:** Once study design quality is controlled, the overall effect of music training is **null (g ≈ 0)** and highly consistent. Effects were only found in studies with no random allocation and non-active controls (g ≈ 0.200).
- **Conclusion:** "Researchers' optimism about the benefits of music training is empirically unjustified."

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Music listening:** Ritter & Ferguson (2017) found positive effects on divergent thinking but Threadgold et al. (2019) found negative effects on convergent thinking. The picture is task-dependent and not uniformly positive.
- **Musical training:** Sala & Gobet's (2020) rigorous meta-analysis finds null effects once study quality is controlled. Cross-sectional advantages of musicians may reflect pre-existing differences (self-selection bias).
- The Ritter & Ferguson result did not survive Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons.
- Music's effect on creativity may be mediated primarily through mood induction (positive affect) rather than a direct music-specific pathway.

#### Mechanism Summary
**Music listening:** Happy music likely enhances creativity through mood induction (positive, activating affect), which maps onto the Baas et al. (2008) finding that activating positive emotions enhance divergent thinking. The effect may not be music-specific but rather a positive affect pathway.
**Musical training:** Cross-sectional advantages of musicians may reflect enhanced working memory and verbal processing, but causal evidence from randomized training studies is null. Any benefits may stem from pre-existing traits that predispose both musical interest and creative thinking.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **2** | Music listening: single study (Ritter 2017) with marginal significance + contradicted by Threadgold 2019 for convergent thinking. Musical training: null effects in rigorous meta-analysis (Sala & Gobet 2020). |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **2** | d ≈ 0.55 for happy music on DT (Ritter 2017), but did not survive multiple comparison correction. Musical training: g ≈ 0 in controlled meta-analysis. Overall evidence for a reliable, meaningful effect is weak. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **5** (listening) / **2** (playing) | Listening to happy music is trivially easy. Learning an instrument requires significant time investment. Scoring for the easier variant (listening). |

### N-2: Visual Art Practice — Drawing/Sketching (視覚芸術の実践)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Schlegel et al. (2015)**
- **Citation:** Schlegel, A., et al. (2015). The artist emerges: Visual art learning alters neural structure and function. *NeuroImage*, 105, 440-451.
- **Design:** Longitudinal comparison of art students vs. non-art students over 3 months. DTI brain imaging + creativity assessments.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Art students showed reorganization of prefrontal white matter associated with increased creativity.
  - Three months of painting training enhanced sketching abilities and **visual divergent thinking**.
  - Did NOT affect perceptual skills — the benefit was specific to creative cognition.
  - Inverse correlation between frontal white matter FA and divergent thinking scores.
- **Limitation:** Not a randomized trial (self-selected art students). Small sample.

**Paper 2: Andrade (2010) — Doodling Study**
- **Citation:** Andrade, J. (2010). What does doodling do? *Applied Cognitive Psychology*, 24(1), 100-106.
- **Design:** 40 participants monitored a monotonous telephone message. Half were assigned to shade printed shapes (doodling condition) while listening.
- **Key Findings:** Doodling group recalled **29% more information** on a surprise memory test (M = 7.5 vs. M = 5.8 correct items).
- **Important note:** This study measured **memory/attention**, NOT creativity per se. It suggests doodling maintains cognitive engagement and reduces daydreaming during boring tasks.
- **Replication concern:** Boggs et al. (2017) did NOT replicate the doodling benefit; structured doodling showed no effect, and unstructured doodling actually hindered performance.

**Paper 3: Nature (2025) — Neural Mechanisms of Visual Arts and Creativity**
- **Citation:** (2025). Neural mechanisms underpinning the association between visual arts education and creativity. *npj Science of Learning*.
- **Design:** Comparison of design-major students vs. non-design peers using creativity assessments and neuroimaging.
- **Key Findings:** Design-major students showed enhanced creative performance and more efficient neural processes related to creativity.
- **Limitation:** Cross-sectional — cannot establish causation. Design students may have pre-existing creative aptitude.

**Paper 4: Crystal Bridges Experiment (Bowen et al., 2014) — VTS Study**
- **Citation:** Bowen, D. H., et al. (2014). Learning to think critically: A visual art experiment.
- **Design:** RCT with 3,811 students randomly assigned to museum visits using Visual Thinking Strategies (VTS) vs. control.
- **Key Findings:** Treatment students demonstrated significantly stronger critical thinking skills including observation, interpretation, flexible thinking, and evidence-based reasoning. Effects were larger for disadvantaged students.
- **Limitation:** Measured critical thinking, not creativity per se. VTS involves viewing/discussing art, not creating it.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Causal evidence for transfer to non-art creativity is limited.** Most studies are cross-sectional (art students vs. non-art students).
- Only one aspect of transfer has reliable causal evidence from RCTs: **observational skills** (VTS studies).
- Andrade (2010) doodling findings were not replicated by Boggs et al. (2017).
- A review concludes: "there is no reason to think that arts education will make children more creative unless the arts are taught in a way that really pushes children to explore and invent."
- Benefits appear to be largely **domain-specific** (visual/spatial creativity) rather than domain-general.

#### Mechanism Summary
Visual art practice engages spatial visualization, flexible perspective-taking, and non-verbal problem-solving. Sustained art training appears to reorganize prefrontal white matter in ways associated with enhanced divergent thinking. However, the benefits are primarily domain-specific (visual/verbal DT) rather than broadly transferable. The act of drawing externalizes thinking, making abstract ideas concrete and manipulable, which may facilitate creative problem-solving in visual domains.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **2** | One longitudinal neuroimaging study (Schlegel 2015, non-randomized), cross-sectional comparisons, one RCT on viewing art (VTS). No RCTs on drawing/sketching practice and creativity transfer. |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **2** | Schlegel shows neural changes and DT improvement but domain-specific. Andrade shows 29% memory improvement (not creativity) and was not replicated. Transfer to non-art creativity is not demonstrated with clear effect sizes. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **4** | Drawing/sketching requires minimal materials (pencil and paper). Some initial self-consciousness, but no significant cost or training to begin basic practice. |

## Category O: Environment & Space Design (環境・空間設計) — NEW CATEGORY

---

### O-1: Moderate Ambient Noise / Café Noise (適度な環境ノイズ)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Mehta, Zhu & Cheema (2012) — The Café Noise Study**
- **Citation:** Mehta, R., Zhu, R., & Cheema, A. (2012). Is noise always bad? Exploring the effects of ambient noise on creative cognition. *Journal of Consumer Research*, 39(4), 784-799.
- **Design:** 5 experiments testing low (50 dB), moderate (70 dB), and high (85 dB) ambient noise levels on creative task performance.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Moderate noise (70 dB, ≈ café level) **enhanced** creative task performance vs. low noise (50 dB).
  - High noise (85 dB) **impaired** creativity.
  - Inverted-U relationship between noise level and creativity.
  - Mechanism: moderate noise increases processing difficulty → abstract/higher-level construal → enhanced creative cognition.
  - Also increased buying likelihood of innovative products.
- **Effect Size:** Specific Cohen's d not reported in accessible sources, but the effect was consistent across 5 experiments.

**Paper 2: Thinking Skills & Creativity (2022) — Partial Replication**
- **Citation:** (2022). What can you do with a bottle and a hanger? Students with high cognitive flexibility give more ideas in the presence of ambient noise. *Thinking Skills and Creativity*.
- **Design:** Experimental study on ambient noise and creativity with cognitive flexibility as moderator.
- **Key Findings:** Participants gave **more ideas** in the presence of ambient noise vs. silence, but ideas were **NOT more original**. The effect was moderated by cognitive flexibility — only students with high cognitive flexibility benefited.
- **Significance:** Partial support only. Noise increases fluency but not originality, and only for some individuals.

**Paper 3: Scientific Reports (2022) — White Noise Study**
- **Citation:** (2022). Cognitive performance, creativity and stress levels of neurotypical young adults under different white noise levels. *Scientific Reports*.
- **Design:** White noise at different levels.
- **Key Findings:** 45 dB white noise (lower than Mehta et al.'s 70 dB) resulted in better cognitive performance, enhanced creativity, and lower stress. This contrasts with Mehta et al.'s finding that 70 dB is the sweet spot.

**Paper 4: Classroom Noise and Children — CONTRADICTORY**
- **Citation:** Study on classroom noise and children's creativity.
- **Key Finding:** No conditions in which classroom noise promoted children's creativity. Some negative effects observed. Children with good selective attention skills were protected against noise effects.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **No formal direct replication failure found**, but follow-up studies show **highly inconsistent results.**
- The optimal noise level varies across studies (45 dB vs. 70 dB), suggesting no robust "sweet spot."
- Benefits appear moderated by individual differences (cognitive flexibility, attention skills, age).
- Type of noise matters: multi-talker ambient noise ≠ white noise ≠ music. The original study used multi-talker noise, making generalization to "café noise" apps uncertain.
- In children, ambient noise appears harmful or null for creativity.
- The mechanism (processing disfluency → abstract construal) is theoretically elegant but may not be robust across populations and contexts.

#### Mechanism Summary
Moderate ambient noise increases processing difficulty (disfluency), which triggers a shift to more abstract, higher-level construal processing. Abstract processing is associated with enhanced creativity because it promotes relational and integrative thinking. However, this mechanism appears fragile — too much noise overwhelms cognitive resources, and the optimal level varies by individual and context.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **3** | Original study is well-designed (5 experiments) and published in a top journal. However, follow-up studies show mixed results. No definitive replication. Individual difference moderators suggest the effect is not universal. |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **2** | Effects on fluency but not originality in partial replication. Optimal noise level inconsistent across studies. The practical effect on real creative output is uncertain. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **5** | Go to a café, or use free apps (Coffitivity, Noisli) to generate ambient noise. Trivially easy, zero cost. |

### O-2: Physical Environment Change (物理的環境の変化)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Meyers-Levy & Zhu (2007) — Ceiling Height Study**
- **Citation:** Meyers-Levy, J., & Zhu, R. (2007). The influence of ceiling height: The effect of priming on the type of processing that people use. *Journal of Consumer Research*, 34(2), 174-186.
- **Design:** 3 experiments. Participants placed in rooms with high ceilings (10 ft / 3.05 m) vs. low ceilings (8 ft / 2.44 m). Tasks included anagram solving, categorization, and memory recall.
- **Key Findings:**
  - High ceilings primed concepts of "freedom" → relational, abstract, integrative processing.
  - Low ceilings primed "confinement" → item-specific, concrete, detail-focused processing.
  - High ceiling participants solved freedom-related anagrams faster and thought more abstractly.
- **Important caveat:** Ceiling height effects required the ceiling height to be made **salient** (e.g., via lantern placement). Without salience manipulation, the effect may not emerge.
- **Effect Size:** Not reported as standardized effect sizes in accessible sources.

**Paper 2: Mehta & Zhu (2009) — Color Effects (Blue vs. Red)**
- **Citation:** Mehta, R., & Zhu, R. (2009). Blue or red? Exploring the effect of color on cognitive task performances. *Science*, 323(5918), 1226-1229.
- **Design:** 6 experiments (total N ≈ 666). Red vs. blue environmental color on cognitive tasks.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Red enhanced detail-oriented tasks (memory, proofreading) — up to 31% improvement.
  - Blue enhanced creative tasks (brainstorming) — participants produced **twice as many creative outputs**.
  - Cohen's d values: 1.1, 0.67, 0.48, 0.57 across different comparisons.
- **REPLICATION FAILURE:** A direct replication in *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review* (with 3x the sample size) failed to find the color-priming effect. Francis (2013) noted the original results may have been "too good to be true" given the sample sizes.

**Paper 3: Oppezzo & Schwartz (2014) — Walking = Environment Change**
- **Citation:** Oppezzo, M., & Schwartz, D. L. (2014). Give your ideas some legs: The positive effect of walking on creative thinking. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 40(4), 1142-1152.
- **Design:** 4 experiments, 176 participants. Walking (indoor/outdoor) vs. sitting on divergent and convergent thinking.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Walking increased divergent thinking creativity by **60% on average.**
  - **81% of participants** showed improved creativity while walking.
  - Effect occurred for **both indoor (treadmill) and outdoor walking** — suggesting the physical act of moving, not the novelty of environment, drives the effect.
  - Residual creative boost when sitting down after walking.
  - NO improvement on convergent thinking.
- **Effect Size:** ~60% average improvement, 81% of participants improved (very large for a behavioral intervention).

**Paper 4: Scoping Review — Physical Environments and Creativity (2023)**
- **Citation:** (2023). Relationships between physical environments and creativity: A scoping review. *ScienceDirect*.
- **Key Findings:**
  - 15 physical attributes identified as relevant to creativity (light, furniture, visual stimulation, indoor plants, windows, privacy, proximity, air quality, sounds, volume, spatial variety, sense of control, etc.).
  - Visual stimulation and social space were the most significant physical aspects for creative output.
  - Natural environments with high/medium perceived naturalness enhanced creative performance.
  - Unconventional spaces did NOT always facilitate creativity — could cause cognitive fixation when ideas could be drawn from the immediate environment. The novelty may wear off over time.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Ceiling height study** (Meyers-Levy & Zhu 2007): Required ceiling height to be made salient; unclear if the effect occurs naturally. Replication status uncertain.
- **Color study** (Mehta & Zhu 2009): Directly failed to replicate in a well-powered follow-up. The original effect sizes (d = 1.1) were likely inflated. Francis (2013) noted statistical concerns.
- **INSEAD workspace study:** Unconventional workspaces did NOT always help and sometimes inhibited creativity (cognitive fixation). The perceived unconventionality is what matters, and it habituates over time.
- Walking benefits (Oppezzo & Schwartz 2014) appear robust but may be more about physical movement than environment change per se.
- Overall, the environment-creativity literature has significant replication concerns and many small-sample studies with large reported effects.

#### Mechanism Summary
Physical environment changes can affect creativity through multiple pathways: (1) **priming** of abstract vs. concrete processing through spatial cues (ceiling height, openness); (2) **novelty exposure** that disrupts routine cognitive patterns; (3) **physical movement** that activates the default mode network; (4) **visual stimulation** that provides new inputs for associative thinking. However, the evidence is mixed, and many specific effects (color, ceiling height) have replication concerns. The most robust finding is that walking (physical movement + environment change) enhances divergent thinking.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability (信頼度) | **2** | Ceiling height and color studies have replication concerns. Walking study is more robust but may not be environment-specific. Scoping reviews confirm the field is underdeveloped with few rigorous experiments. INSEAD study found unconventional workspaces can backfire. |
| Effect Size (効果量) | **3** | Walking shows large effects (60% improvement), but specific environment-only effects (ceiling height, color, workspace novelty) are uncertain. Scoring at medium to account for the walking component vs. the weaker environment-specific evidence. |
| Ease of Adoption (取り入れやすさ) | **4** | Going to a different location (café, library, park) is easy. Changing workspace arrangement is minor effort. Major environmental redesign (ceiling height, color schemes) requires more investment. Scoring for the easier variants. |

## Key Takeaways for Categories K-O

### Strongest Evidence (Highest Reliability)
1. **K-1: Positive emotion cultivation** — Backed by a comprehensive meta-analysis (Baas et al. 2008, 102 effect sizes) and classic experiments (Isen et al. 1987). The finding that activating positive emotions enhance divergent thinking is well-established.
2. **M-1: AUT/DT practice** — Three meta-analyses confirm that creativity training works (Δ = 0.64), though transfer to real-world creativity is less certain.

### Largest Reported Effect Sizes
1. **O-2: Environment change** (specifically walking) — 60% average improvement in divergent thinking (Oppezzo & Schwartz 2014), though this may be more about physical movement than environment.
2. **M-1: AUT practice** — Meta-analytic Δ = 0.64, though publication-bias-adjusted estimates are 0.29-0.32.

### Most Concerning Replication Issues
1. **L-2: Daydreaming** — Baird et al. (2012) has faced multiple replication failures (Smeekens & Kane 2016, Murray 2021).
2. **O-2: Environment change** — Mehta & Zhu (2009) color effects failed to replicate. Ceiling height effects require artificial salience.
3. **N-1: Music** — Ritter & Ferguson (2017) finding did not survive multiple comparison correction; Threadgold (2019) found music impairs convergent creativity.

### New Categories Assessment
- **Category N (Music & Art):** Surprisingly weak evidence base when examined rigorously. Music listening benefits may be entirely attributable to positive mood induction (K-1). Musical training shows null causal effects in rigorous meta-analysis. Visual art practice shows domain-specific neural changes but limited transfer evidence.
- **Category O (Environment & Space Design):** Mixed evidence. The ambient noise finding is appealing but insufficiently replicated and moderated by individual differences. Physical environment change effects are mostly underpowered or have replication concerns, with the notable exception of the walking studies (which overlap with Category A).
