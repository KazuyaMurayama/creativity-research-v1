## Category A: Physical Exercise

---

### A-1: Walking

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Oppezzo & Schwartz (2014)**
- *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 40(4), 1142-1152
- "Give Your Ideas Some Legs: The Positive Effect of Walking on Creative Thinking"
- Design: 4 experiments, N=176, within-subjects and between-subjects
- Key finding: Walking increased divergent thinking (Guilford's Alternate Uses) by 81% of participants; average creative output increased ~60% over sitting. Residual creative boost persisted after sitting back down.
- Effect on convergent thinking (Compound Remote Associates): only 23% improvement -- NOT a general cognitive boost, specific to divergent thinking.
- Indoor treadmill walking was nearly as effective as outdoor walking.

**Paper 2: Kuo & Yeh (2016)**
- *Frontiers in Psychology*, "Sensorimotor-Conceptual Integration in Free Walking Enhances Divergent Thinking for Young and Older Adults"
- Design: Experimental, undergraduates at National Taiwan University
- Key finding: Free-path walking outperformed constrained rectangular-path walking on fluency, flexibility, and originality of the Alternate Uses Task. Effect replicated in older adults. Suggests sensorimotor-conceptual integration is the mechanism, not mere locomotion.

**Paper 3: Murali & Handel (2022)**
- *Psychological Research*, "Motor restrictions impair divergent thinking during walking and during sitting"
- Key finding: AUT scores were higher during walking than sitting. Unrestricted movement broadened visual/perceptual attention, which facilitated broadened conceptual attention and more original ideas.

**Paper 4: Aga et al. (2021)**
- *Brain Sciences*, 11(8), 1046, "Regular Vigorous-Intensity Physical Activity and Walking Are Associated with Divergent but not Convergent Thinking in Japanese Young Adults"
- Design: Cross-sectional, young adults
- Key finding: Regular walking was positively associated with novelty of divergent thinking. Only walking (not moderate/vigorous PA) predicted novelty -- unique value of walking pace.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Bartholomae (2024)**: Controlled treadmill experiment distinguishing stationary, unobstructed, and obstructed walking found NO significant differences in creativity scores. Suggests the benefit may come from broader motor freedom (head, eyes, arms) rather than leg movement per se.
- **Colzato et al. (2013)**: Moderate-to-intense cycling IMPAIRED divergent thinking flexibility in non-athletes. Walking-pace is critical -- intensity matters.

#### Mechanism Summary
Walking at a natural pace broadens attentional focus (both perceptual and conceptual), reduces top-down cognitive control, and activates default mode network (DMN). Free, unconstrained walking enhances sensorimotor-conceptual integration. The effect is specific to divergent (not convergent) thinking. Possible role of BDNF release and prefrontal cortex blood flow.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **4** | Multiple experimental studies across labs (Oppezzo, Kuo, Murali, Aga); replicated internationally; but one 2024 controlled study challenges the mechanism; no formal meta-analysis specific to walking alone |
| Effect Size | **5** | Oppezzo reports 60% increase in creative output, 81% of participants improved; these are large effects (d likely > 0.8); consistent across studies |
| Ease of Adoption | **5** | Zero cost, zero equipment, can start immediately; works on treadmill or outdoors |

### A-2: Regular Exercise

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Rominger et al. (2022) -- META-ANALYSIS**
- *Sports Medicine - Open*, 8, 62
- "Acute and Chronic Physical Activity Increases Creative Ideation Performance: A Systematic Review and Multilevel Meta-analysis"
- Design: Multilevel meta-analysis of 35 studies (7 cross-sectional, 28 intervention); 115 effect sizes from intervention studies; 2,073 participants (cross-sectional), 1,624 participants (intervention)
- **Key effect sizes**:
  - Cross-sectional: r = 0.22 (SE = 0.06, p = 0.002, 95% CI [0.10-0.34]) -- medium
  - Intervention: Hedges' g = 0.47 (SE = 0.09, p < 0.001, 95% CI [0.30-0.65]) -- medium
- Chronic interventions showed stronger effects than single-bout acute interventions
- Publication bias detected but effect remained similar after trimming (g still significant)

**Paper 2: Vasilopoulos et al. (2023)**
- *Educational Psychology Review*, Multi-level meta-analysis
- Design: 92 RCTs, children aged 5-12, N = 25,334
- Key finding: Physical activity interventions improved creativity with g = 0.70 (moderate-to-large). Aerobic and/or moderate-to-vigorous PA was most beneficial.

**Paper 3: Rominger et al. (2024)**
- *Scientific Reports*, "Habitual physical activity is related to more creative activities and achievements"
- Design: Ecological study, N=156, 5-day mobile sensor wear
- Key finding: Less sedentary behavior and more moderate-to-vigorous PA associated with more frequent real-life creative activities and achievements. Extends lab findings to ecological validity.

**Paper 4: Chen et al. (2024)**
- *Discover Psychology*, "Exploring the impact of acute physical activity on creative thinking: a comprehensive narrative review"
- Key finding: Natural walking significantly enhances divergent thinking. Suggestive evidence that moderate-intensity aerobic exercise and dance positively influence divergent thinking. High-intensity exercise may impair creativity.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Colzato et al. (2013)**: Intense cycling significantly impaired divergent thinking flexibility in non-athletes. Athletes showed a nonsignificant benefit. Intensity is a critical moderator.
- High heterogeneity in intervention studies (63% between-study variance)
- Most moderators (type, domain, population) failed to reach significance -- possibly due to power limitations
- The causal direction in cross-sectional data is unclear (creative people may exercise more, not the reverse)

#### Mechanism Summary
Exercise improves executive control, attention, and memory -- cognitive foundations of creative thinking. Moderate aerobic exercise may facilitate an optimal arousal state for divergent thinking. Chronic exercise builds long-term cognitive reserve. The relationship follows an inverted-U pattern: moderate intensity is optimal; high intensity may impair creativity by exhausting executive resources.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **5** | Meta-analysis (Rominger 2022) plus another meta-analysis (Vasilopoulos 2023); 35+ studies; 92 RCTs in children; consistent medium effects across populations |
| Effect Size | **3** | Hedges' g = 0.47 (medium) for interventions; r = 0.22 for cross-sectional; g = 0.70 in children; consistently medium range |
| Ease of Adoption | **4** | Requires some habit formation; any form of moderate exercise works; minor schedule adjustment needed |

### A-3: Dance / Whole-Body Movement (NEW)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Richard et al. (2021)**
- "Effects of Movement Improvisation and Aerobic Dancing on Motor Creativity and Divergent Thinking"
- Design: RCT, N=92 college students, 3 groups (movement improvisation, aerobic dance, control), 10 sessions over 5 weeks
- Key finding: Significant effect on motor fluency and flexibility; movement improvisation yielded greatest effects, followed by aerobic dancing, then control. However, effects on standard cognitive divergent thinking (figural DT) were non-significant for the condition x time interaction. Cohen's d was low for improvisation vs. other conditions on figural DT.

**Paper 2: Sowden et al. (2015) / Lewis & Lovatt (2013)**
- "Improvisation Facilitates Divergent Thinking and Creativity: Realizing a Benefit of Primary School Arts Education"
- Design: Two experiments with primary school children; improvised vs. non-improvised dance/acting
- Key finding: In both experiments, children who participated in improvisation interventions showed better divergent thinking and creativity after the intervention (Instances Task, Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking figural activity).

**Paper 3: Frontiers in Human Neuroscience (2024)**
- "Embodied metacognition as strengthened functional connection between neural correlates of metacognition and dance in dancers"
- Design: Neuroimaging study
- Key finding: Extended dance training enhances the connection between neural correlates of metacognition and dance, which may serve as the neural predisposition for fostering general creativity performance in dancers.

**Paper 4: Felsman et al. (2020)**
- *Journal of Creative Behavior* (via ScienceDirect)
- Design: RCT -- one of the largest randomized experiments on improvisation
- Key finding: Improv training improved divergent thinking (replicating Lewis & Lovatt 2013) and also increased uncertainty tolerance and affective well-being. Causal evidence from randomized design.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- Effect sizes on standard cognitive divergent thinking (AUT, TTCT) tend to be low-to-moderate; strongest effects are on motor creativity measures (fluency, flexibility of movement) rather than general cognitive creativity.
- Most studies with children; fewer rigorous adult RCTs.
- Difficult to separate the "improvisation" component from the "movement" component -- improvised dance works better than choreographed dance, suggesting the improvisation aspect matters more than the physical movement.
- Quasi-experimental designs dominate the adult literature.

#### Mechanism Summary
Dance, especially improvisational dance, engages embodied cognition -- the body becomes a cognitive medium for thinking. The mirror neuron system (extended MNS) facilitates movement creation and engages higher-level cognitive areas. Movement improvisation integrates low-level perception-action coupling with high-level creative cognition. Free/unconstrained movement broadens both perceptual and conceptual attention. The improvisational element (not just the physical movement) appears critical for creativity benefits.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **3** | Few RCTs with adult populations; more evidence in children; quasi-experimental designs dominate; effects on cognitive DT are weaker than on motor creativity |
| Effect Size | **2** | Low-to-moderate effects on standard cognitive divergent thinking; stronger for motor creativity; Cohen's d low for figural DT in Richard et al. |
| Ease of Adoption | **3** | Requires learning, finding classes or instruction; dance improvisation is more accessible than choreographed forms; some people may have psychological barriers |

## Category B: Sleep & Rest

---

### B-1: Sufficient Sleep (REM)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Cai, Mednick et al. (2009)**
- *PNAS*, 106(25), 10130-10134
- "REM, not incubation, improves creativity by priming associative networks"
- Design: Nap paradigm, N=77 (across conditions), between-subjects (REM nap vs. NREM nap vs. quiet rest)
- **Key finding**: REM group improved by ~40% on primed Remote Associates Test items vs. morning baseline. NREM and quiet rest groups showed NO improvement. Total sleep time was not correlated with improvement (r = 0.104, p = 0.53) -- it was REM quality, not duration, that mattered.
- P = 0.047 for REM vs. other groups (1-way ANOVA + post hoc)
- REM did NOT improve non-primed items (p = 0.261) -- requires prior exposure to problem elements

**Paper 2: Wagner et al. (2004)**
- *Nature*, 427, 352-355
- "Sleep inspires insight"
- Design: N=66, sleep vs. wake conditions
- Key finding: Subjects who slept were more than twice as likely to discover a hidden shortcut rule in a number reduction task compared to those who stayed awake. 59% of sleepers gained insight vs. 23% of non-sleepers.

**Paper 3: Lewis, Knoblich & Poe (2018)**
- *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 22(6), 491-503
- "How Memory Replay in Sleep Boosts Creative Problem-Solving"
- Design: Review/theoretical integration
- Key finding: NREM replay extracts rules/gist; REM replay enables novel associations. The NREM-REM cycle iteratively restructures knowledge across a night of sleep, supporting creative insight.

**Paper 4: Systematic Review (2024)**
- *Creativity Research Journal* (Taylor & Francis)
- "The Effect of Sleep Deprivation on Creative Cognition: A Systematic Review"
- Key finding: 8 studies met inclusion criteria. Horne (1988) found sleep deprivation impaired ALL aspects of Torrance Tests (figural and verbal) with "very large effect sizes." Overall, sleep deprivation reliably impairs creative cognition, but the evidence base is smaller than expected. Study quality rated "poor" to "fair."

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- Small sample sizes dominate the field
- Few studies report formal effect sizes
- Cai et al. (2009): REM benefit only occurred for previously-primed items -- no general creativity boost without prior exposure
- Partial replication by Carlsson et al.: The specific priming hypothesis was not fully supported, though REM group still improved more than rest group
- The broader sleep-creativity literature is "unexpectedly scarce" compared to sleep-memory literature
- 2024 systematic review: study quality generally "poor to fair"; methods too diverse for formal meta-analysis

#### Mechanism Summary
During REM sleep, cholinergic and noradrenergic neuromodulation loosens associative networks, allowing normally unconnected semantic concepts to form new links. NREM sleep extracts rules and consolidates gist; REM sleep creates novel associations. The NREM-REM cycle across a full night iteratively restructures knowledge. Sleep deprivation impairs prefrontal cortex function (prefrontal vulnerability hypothesis), disrupting executive functions critical to divergent thinking.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **3** | Key studies (Cai 2009, Wagner 2004) are well-cited; systematic review exists but with small number of studies (8); no meta-analysis specific to sleep-creativity; replication is partial; study quality concerns |
| Effect Size | **4** | ~40% improvement (Cai 2009); 2x insight rate (Wagner 2004); sleep deprivation shows "very large" impairments (Horne 1988); consistently medium-large when measured |
| Ease of Adoption | **4** | Most people can prioritize sleep; some lifestyle adjustments needed; free; but sleep quality is not always under conscious control |

### B-2: Hypnagogic Naps (N1 Sleep)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Lacaux et al. (2021)**
- *Science Advances*, 7(50), eabj5866
- "Sleep onset is a creative sweet spot"
- Design: N=103, polysomnography-monitored rest periods; mathematical problem with hidden rule
- **Key finding**: Spending at least 15 seconds in N1 sleep TRIPLED the chance of discovering the hidden rule (83% vs. 30% for those who stayed awake). The effect VANISHED if subjects reached N2 (deeper sleep) -- subjects in N2 were ~6x less likely to discover the rule than N1 subjects. After an average of only ~1 minute in N1, most subjects (83%) suddenly discovered the hidden rule.
- Brain signature: Specific pattern of alpha and delta waves during hypnagogic state predicted creative breakthroughs.

**Paper 2: Haar Horowitz et al. (2023) -- MIT Media Lab**
- *Scientific Reports*, "Targeted dream incubation at sleep onset increases post-sleep creative performance"
- Design: Nap with Targeted Dream Incubation (TDI) vs. nap without TDI vs. staying awake
- Key finding: TDI nap group performed 43% more creatively than nap-only group and 78% more creatively than awake group. Participants who napped showed higher semantic distance (broader associative divergence) than awake participants.

**Paper 3: Historical Accounts (Edison, Dali)**
- Thomas Edison held steel balls while dozing in a chair; when they dropped, the noise woke him and he recorded his ideas.
- Salvador Dali held a key over a plate; the clang upon dropping woke him from hypnagogia.
- Both systematically used the N1-to-waking transition as a creative technique.
- These historical accounts were validated by the Lacaux (2021) study.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- The "sweet spot" is narrow and difficult to control -- falling too deeply asleep eliminates the benefit
- Only one major controlled study (Lacaux 2021) plus one TDI study (MIT 2023)
- The mathematical insight task (hidden rule) is a specific type of creativity; generalizability to other creative domains is assumed but not fully tested
- Requires ability to fall asleep on demand in N1 and wake up before N2, which is a skill few people have naturally
- Requires holding an object (Edison technique) or using technology (Dormio device at MIT) to catch the N1 window

#### Mechanism Summary
N1 sleep represents an "ideal cocktail for creativity": it simultaneously engages the default mode network (spontaneous thinking) and cognitive control networks. N1 is accompanied by involuntary, spontaneous, dream-like perceptual experiences (hypnagogic imagery) that incorporate recent experiences with loosely associated memories. Executive control relaxes enough to permit novel associations, but consciousness is maintained enough to encode insights. The transition from N1 back to wakefulness preserves these associations for conscious access.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **2** | Only 2 controlled studies (Lacaux 2021, MIT 2023); strong effect in those studies but limited replication; historical accounts are anecdotal; single creative task type |
| Effect Size | **5** | Tripled insight rate (83% vs. 30%); 43-78% improvement in TDI study; among the largest effect sizes in the entire creativity literature |
| Ease of Adoption | **3** | Requires learning the Edison/Dali technique; needs a quiet rest period; technically free; but difficult to control sleep onset precisely; requires practice |

## Category C: Mindfulness & Meditation

---

### C-1: Open-Monitoring Meditation

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Colzato, Ozturk & Hommel (2012)**
- *Frontiers in Psychology*, 3, 116
- "Meditate to Create: The Impact of Focused-Attention and Open-Monitoring Training on Convergent and Divergent Thinking"
- Design: Within-subjects, experienced meditators; OM vs. FA meditation followed by AUT (divergent) and RAT (convergent)
- Key finding: OM meditation specifically promoted divergent thinking (fluency, flexibility, originality on AUT). FA meditation did NOT sustain convergent thinking (weak/non-significant effects on RAT). OM meditation induced a cognitive-control state characterized by weak, distributed top-down control, facilitating free-flowing ideation.

**Paper 2: Hughes, Ball, Richardson & Judge (2023) -- META-ANALYSIS**
- *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 30, 2155-2186
- "A meta-analytical review of the impact of mindfulness on creativity: Framing current lines of research and defining moderator variables"
- Design: Two meta-analyses -- 20 control-group studies, 17 pretest-posttest studies
- **Key effect sizes**:
  - Control group designs: d = 0.42 (95% CI [0.29, 0.54])
  - Pretest-posttest designs: d = 0.59 (95% CI [0.38, 0.81])
- Significant moderators: intervention length, creativity task type (divergent vs. convergent), control group type
- Overall: more advantageous outcomes for convergent (vs. divergent) thinking tasks -- counterintuitively, but may reflect the wider mindfulness literature including FA meditation studies

**Paper 3: Lippelt, Hommel & Colzato (2014)**
- *Frontiers in Cognition*, "Focused attention, open monitoring and loving kindness meditation: effects on attention, conflict monitoring, and creativity -- A review"
- Key finding: Convergent thinking improved after FA meditation; divergent thinking significantly enhanced after OM meditation. The three meditation types (FA, OM, loving-kindness) have dissociable effects on cognition. FA and OM induce opposite cognitive-control states that support state-compatible thinking styles.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- Few studies have adopted RCT designs; most use experienced meditators (selection bias)
- Replication concerns: since Colzato et al. (2012), relatively little additional evidence specifically testing OM vs. FA effects on divergent vs. convergent thinking
- Mood may be a confounding variable: elevated mood from meditation facilitates divergent thinking but may interfere with convergent thinking
- The Hughes (2023) meta-analysis found the overall mindfulness-creativity effect was more advantageous for convergent thinking, which complicates the OM-divergent narrative
- Intervention length varies widely across studies, making comparisons difficult

#### Mechanism Summary
OM meditation cultivates a cognitive-control state with weak, distributed top-down control. This allows spontaneous thoughts to arise without habitual verbal-conceptual processing or judgment. The reduced self-censoring facilitates fluency, flexibility, and originality. OM meditation engages the default mode network while maintaining metacognitive awareness. Positive mood induced by meditation may also contribute to broadened attentional scope (consistent with Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory).

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **4** | Meta-analysis (Hughes 2023) with d=0.42-0.59 across 37 studies; seminal study (Colzato 2012) widely cited; consistent direction of effects; but few pure OM-specific RCTs |
| Effect Size | **3** | d = 0.42 (control groups) to d = 0.59 (pre-post) -- medium effect sizes; consistent but not large |
| Ease of Adoption | **4** | Free; can start with guided meditation apps; OM meditation is slightly harder to learn than FA; requires daily practice of 15-25 minutes; minor learning curve |

### C-2: Focused-Attention Meditation

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Colzato et al. (2012)** (same paper as C-1)
- Key finding for FA: FA meditation did NOT sustain convergent thinking in this specific study. The effect was weak and non-significant for RAT. The authors suggested mood enhancement from meditation may have counteracted potential convergent thinking benefits.

**Paper 2: Lippelt et al. (2014)** (review)
- More nuanced finding: "While convergent thinking tended to improve after FA meditation, divergent thinking was significantly enhanced after OM meditation." The two types of meditation affected thinking in opposite ways.
- However, Colzato et al. (2017) follow-up noted the FA-convergent effect was weak and not reliably replicable.

**Paper 3: MDPI (2025)**
- "Examining the Impact of Brief Mindfulness Practice on Sustained Attention, Attentional Inhibition and Convergent Thinking"
- Focus on how sustained attention and attentional inhibition (core FA meditation outcomes) enable convergent creative problem-solving (rebus puzzles).

**Paper 4: Lebuda et al. (2016) / Baas et al. review**
- Only the "observe" facet of mindfulness (related to OM, not FA) reliably predicted creativity. FA-related facets (describe, act with awareness, non-reactivity) showed weaker or null relationships.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- The FA-convergent thinking link is the weakest finding in this literature
- Colzato et al. (2017) found the effect was not replicable
- Mood may confound: FA meditation improves mood, but elevated mood may actually interfere with convergent thinking
- Much less evidence specifically for FA meditation and creativity compared to OM
- The overall mindfulness meta-analysis (Hughes 2023) aggregates across meditation types, making FA-specific conclusions difficult

#### Mechanism Summary
FA meditation strengthens sustained attention and attentional inhibition -- cognitive processes needed for the convergent phase of creative problem-solving (evaluating and selecting the single best solution). However, the creativity benefit of FA meditation is weak and inconsistent. FA may primarily serve as a cognitive training tool that supports the evaluation stage of creativity, rather than the idea-generation stage.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **2** | The FA-convergent effect was weak in the original study and not reliably replicable (Colzato 2017); much less specific evidence than OM; subsumed within broader mindfulness meta-analyses |
| Effect Size | **2** | Weak and inconsistent effects; "tended to improve" but not significant in key studies; unclear magnitude |
| Ease of Adoption | **5** | FA meditation is the easiest form to learn (focus on breath/object); widely taught; free; abundant apps and resources |

## Category D: Nature

---

### D-1: Nature Immersion (Multi-Day)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Atchley, Strayer & Atchley (2012)**
- *PLOS ONE*, 7(12), e51474
- "Creativity in the Wild: Improving Creative Reasoning through Immersion in Natural Settings"
- Design: Between-subjects, N=56, Outward Bound expeditions (Alaska, Colorado, Maine, Washington), 4-6 days in wilderness without electronics; RAT administered either pre-hike or on day 4
- **Key finding**: Post-hike group scored 50% higher on RAT (M=6.08/10) vs. pre-hike group (M=4.14/10). F(1,53) = 9.71, p < .01. **Cohen's d = 0.86** (large).
- No electronic devices during the hike period

**Paper 2: Collins et al. (2016); McAnnally et al. (2018); Arbuthnott & Sutter (2019); Bruno (2022)**
- Multiple follow-up studies in wilderness immersion contexts
- All demonstrated increases in creative performance following multi-day nature immersion
- These studies are cited in the 2024 scoping review as converging on the same conclusion

**Paper 3: Ferraro (2015)**
- Follow-up wilderness immersion study
- Consistent with Atchley et al. findings

**Paper 4: Scoping Review (2024)**
- *Educational Psychology Review*, "Exploring the Nature-Creativity Connection Across Different Settings"
- Key finding: 6 of 8 convergent thinking studies (75%) showed significant effects; 5 of 8 divergent thinking studies (63%) showed significant effects. Convergent thinking studies were predominantly in wilderness settings (consistent with Atchley's RAT results); divergent thinking studies were more often indoor/short-duration.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Confound**: Cannot determine if effects are from nature exposure, technology disconnection, physical activity (hiking), social interaction, or reduced stress
- All wilderness studies lack adequate control groups (no group that hikes with electronics, or stays in a cabin without hiking)
- Between-subjects design in Atchley (2012) means individual differences may account for some variance
- Effect may diminish with shorter exposures (the 50% improvement required 4 days)
- Limited to RAT as creativity measure in the seminal study

#### Mechanism Summary
Based on Kaplan's Attention Restoration Theory (ART): natural environments provide "soft fascination" that allows directed attention to recover from fatigue. Restored attention enables better executive function, including creative reasoning. Technology disconnection may reduce cognitive load and attention fragmentation. The default mode network becomes more active during the restful, low-demand nature state, supporting incubation and mind-wandering. Multiple days may be needed for full prefrontal cortex restoration.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **3** | One well-cited study (d=0.86) with multiple conceptual replications (Collins, McAnnally, Ferraro, Arbuthnott, Bruno); but all share the same confounds; no RCTs with adequate controls; scoping review available but no meta-analysis |
| Effect Size | **5** | Cohen's d = 0.86 (large); 50% improvement on RAT; consistent large effects across replications; among the largest reported in the creativity literature |
| Ease of Adoption | **1** | Requires 4+ days in wilderness; significant time/planning/cost investment; major lifestyle disruption; not practical as a regular habit |

### D-2: Daily Nature Contact

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Frontiers in Psychiatry (2022)**
- "The influence of natural environments on creativity"
- Design: N=100 subjects on an urban campus; 3 conditions (high, medium, low perceived naturalness); ATTA (Abbreviated Torrance Test for Adults)
- Key finding: High- and medium-perceived naturalness in the urban campus was superior to low-perceived naturalness for creative performance.

**Paper 2: Palanica et al. (2019)**
- *Journal of Environmental Psychology*
- "A comparison of nature and urban environments on creative thinking across different levels of reality"
- Design: Two experiments -- Exp 1 (indoor viewing via tablet/VR); Exp 2 (real-life outdoor)
- Key finding: Indoors, nature videos facilitated higher creativity than urban videos (across 2D tablet and 3D VR). But outdoors, BOTH nature and urban settings evoked similarly high creativity. Being outside may be sufficient regardless of nature presence.

**Paper 3: Drake & Kam (2025)**
- *PLOS ONE*, "The effect of nature on creativity through mental imagery"
- Design: Within-subjects, N=97, guided mental imagery of nature vs. urban walks
- Key finding: Even a mental walk in nature may benefit convergent thinking (through replenished directed attention). Previous studies reported effect sizes between d = 0.54 and d = 0.86. Study aimed to detect d = 0.3 (conservative). Nature benefits convergent thinking AFTER exposure and divergent thinking DURING exposure.

**Paper 4: Study among Danish Creative Professionals**
- *Landscape and Urban Planning*
- Key finding: Nature enhances creativity by making people more curious, facilitating new ideas, and promoting flexible thinking. Nature plays a role especially in the Preparation and Incubation phases of the creative process.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- Palanica et al. (2019) found that simply being outdoors (even in an urban setting with little greenery) produced similar creativity as being in nature -- complicating the "nature-specific" explanation
- Short-duration effects may be more about attention restoration than creativity per se
- Effect sizes for short nature exposures are smaller (d = 0.3-0.54) than multi-day immersion (d = 0.86)
- The field is still emerging; relatively few studies specifically measure creativity (vs. attention, well-being)
- Indoor nature-viewing effects may not be robust

#### Mechanism Summary
Attention Restoration Theory: even short nature exposure (20-30 minutes in a park, or viewing nature images) provides "soft fascination" that restores directed attention. This freed-up attentional capacity can be redirected toward creative problem-solving. Nature reduces cognitive fatigue from technology and urban stimulation. The relaxation response may facilitate mind-wandering and incubation. However, the effect may not be nature-specific -- simply being in any low-demand outdoor environment may suffice.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **2** | Emerging evidence base; few studies specifically measure creativity (vs. attention); mixed findings on nature-specificity; no meta-analysis for short exposure + creativity |
| Effect Size | **3** | d = 0.3-0.54 estimated from available studies; modest but meaningful; smaller than multi-day immersion |
| Ease of Adoption | **5** | Walk in a nearby park; view nature images; requires no equipment or cost; can be done daily in ~20-30 minutes |

## Category E: Information Gathering

---

### E-1: Cross-Domain Information

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Mannucci & Yong (2018)**
- *Academy of Management Journal*, 61(5), 1741-1763
- "The Differential Impact of Knowledge Depth and Knowledge Breadth on Creativity over Individual Careers"
- Design: Longitudinal study in the Hollywood animation industry
- Key finding: Knowledge breadth and depth have differential effects on creativity depending on career age. Knowledge depth is more beneficial early in careers (increases complexity of knowledge structures). Knowledge breadth is more beneficial later in careers (when knowledge structures have become rigid and need flexibility). Supported by longitudinal data.

**Paper 2: Root-Bernstein & Root-Bernstein (multiple publications)**
- Polymathy and creative genius
- Key finding: Highly creative people (Nobel laureates, etc.) are significantly more likely to have artistic hobbies, cross-domain interests, and polymathic orientations than less creative peers. Conceptualized as "correlative talent" -- the integration of breadth, depth, and synthesis across domains.

**Paper 3: Damian & Simonton (2014)**
- Chapter in *The Wiley Handbook of Genius*
- "Diversifying Experiences in the Development of Genius"
- Key finding: "Diversifying experiences" (unusual, unexpected life experiences including cross-domain exposure) enhance cognitive flexibility, which is a foundation for creative genius. Historiometric analyses of eminent creators consistently find breadth of interest as a predictor of creative eminence.

**Paper 4: Ritter, Damian, Simonton et al. (2012/2020)**
- *Frontiers in Psychology*, "The Effects of an Ecological Diversifying Experience on Creativity"
- Design: Experimental
- Key finding: Diversifying experiences (exposure to unusual, unexpected information) enhanced cognitive flexibility and creative performance. Active involvement is key -- passive exposure alone is insufficient.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Baer (2012, 2016)**: Domain-specificity argument -- creativity in one domain does NOT predict creativity in another. Polymaths exist but are the exception, not the rule. The creative benefits of breadth may be overstated.
- Mannucci & Yong (2018): Knowledge breadth is actually LESS beneficial early in careers; the effect is contingent on career stage
- Most evidence is correlational (historiometric, cross-sectional) rather than experimental
- Difficult to determine whether cross-domain interest causes creativity or creative people naturally seek breadth
- The "jack of all trades, master of none" concern: insufficient depth in any domain may prevent creative contribution

#### Mechanism Summary
Cross-domain information exposure destabilizes existing cognitive schemas, forcing the construction of new integrative frameworks. Remote concepts from different domains can be recombined into novel associations (Mednick's associative theory of creativity). Diversifying experiences enhance cognitive flexibility by creating broader, more interconnected knowledge networks. The process is analogous to Simonton's "chance-configuration theory" where more diverse mental elements increase the probability of novel combinations. However, some domain depth is required as a foundation.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **3** | Mostly historiometric/correlational evidence (Simonton, Root-Bernstein); some experimental support (Ritter et al.); strong longitudinal study (Mannucci & Yong); but few RCTs specifically testing cross-domain information intake |
| Effect Size | **3** | Consistent positive associations in historiometric data; Mannucci & Yong found significant moderating effects; but precise effect sizes are hard to isolate from correlational data; medium estimated |
| Ease of Adoption | **5** | Read articles/books outside your field; listen to diverse podcasts; free and immediately actionable; requires only intentional choice of information sources |

### E-2: Cultural Video Watching

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Leung, Maddux, Galinsky & Chiu (2008)**
- *American Psychologist*, 63(3), 169-181
- "Multicultural Experience Enhances Creativity: The When and How"
- Design: Multiple experimental studies
- Key finding: Extensiveness of multicultural experience positively related to creative performance (insight learning, remote association, idea generation) and creativity-supporting cognitive processes. The first to empirically demonstrate that exposure to multiple cultures can enhance creativity. Effects moderated by openness to experience and need for cognitive closure.

**Paper 2: Leung & Chiu (2010)**
- *Journal of Cross-Cultural Psychology*, 41(5-6), 723-741
- "Multicultural experience, idea receptiveness, and creativity"
- Design: Experimental, cultural priming with visual materials
- Key finding: European American undergraduates exposed to American vs. Chinese vs. fusion cultural visual materials showed enhanced creative writing and creative analogies. Effect mediated by generation of unconventional ideas and receptiveness to foreign cultural ideas. High need for cognitive closure attenuated the effect.

**Paper 3: Chinese College Student Priming Study**
- Design: RCT, N=89, 4 conditions (American culture, dual cultures, Chinese culture, control), 45-minute cultural slides with pictures, music, and videos
- Key finding: American culture priming group scored significantly higher than control on uniqueness and novelty of creative story-writing task. Cultural video watching produced physiological arousal (left-side SMR) and higher brain activity.

**Paper 4: Maddux & Galinsky (2009)**
- *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 96(5), 1047-1061
- "Cultural Borders and Mental Barriers"
- Key finding: Living abroad (not just traveling) predicted creativity on multiple measures. Priming past multicultural experiences temporarily boosted creativity. The depth of cultural engagement matters more than mere exposure.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Depth matters**: Mere passive viewing of foreign culture may be insufficient. Leung & Maddux emphasize that "deep engagement" (understanding WHY the culture does something) is key, not just surface exposure.
- **Need for cognitive closure moderates**: People high in need for closure may actually become LESS creative from multicultural exposure
- **Cheng et al.**: The creativity benefit may be restricted to tasks in specific cultural domains, not domain-general
- No specific effect sizes (Cohen's d) are reported in the cultural priming studies
- The gap between living abroad (strong effect) and watching videos (weaker, less tested) is significant

#### Mechanism Summary
Exposure to foreign cultural concepts expands existing conceptual categories, enabling novel recombinations. Multicultural priming activates unconventional knowledge and increases receptiveness to culturally foreign ideas. The mechanism operates through concept expansion -- encountering how other cultures solve problems/categorize the world loosens functional fixedness. However, active engagement (trying to understand why) is critical; passive viewing produces weaker effects. The effect is moderated by individual differences in openness and cognitive closure.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **3** | Multiple experimental studies from Leung/Maddux/Chiu group; consistent effects; but limited replication by independent groups; no specific effect sizes reported; the video-specific evidence is thinner than the broader multicultural literature |
| Effect Size | **2** | "Significantly higher" but no Cohen's d reported; the effect for video/passive exposure is likely smaller than living abroad; moderated by personality traits; unclear magnitude |
| Ease of Adoption | **5** | Watch cultural documentaries at home; free (YouTube, streaming); requires only intentional viewing choice; can start immediately |

### E-3: Fiction Reading (NEW)

#### Key Papers

**Paper 1: Kidd & Castano (2013)**
- *Science*, 342(6156), 377-380
- "Reading Literary Fiction Improves Theory of Mind"
- Design: 5 experiments, participants randomly assigned to read literary fiction, popular fiction, nonfiction, or nothing
- Key finding: Reading literary fiction (vs. popular fiction or nonfiction) significantly improved performance on Theory of Mind tasks (Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test, Yoni task). Literary fiction's focus on complex inner lives of characters requires active imaginative engagement.
- Note: This is about social cognition, not divergent thinking directly, but ToM is a component of creative perspective-taking.

**Paper 2: Djikic & Oatley (2009, 2013)**
- *Creativity Research Journal*, "On Being Moved by Art: How Reading Fiction Transforms the Self" (2009)
- Design: Experimental, N=100; Chekhov short story vs. matched nonfiction control (same content, documentary form)
- Key finding (2009): Reading literary fiction caused significantly greater change in self-reported personality traits than reading nonfiction. Emotion change mediated the effect.
- Key finding (2013): Reading fiction reduced need for cognitive closure compared to nonfiction essays. Lower need for closure is associated with higher creativity.

**Paper 3: Replication/Extension (2022)**
- *Creativity Research Journal*, "Opening the closed mind? Effects of reading literary fiction on need for closure and creativity"
- Design: Between-subjects, N=111 students, literary fiction vs. nonfictional essays
- Key finding: Aimed to replicate Djikic (2013) and extend to test effects on openness to experience, cognitive complexity, imaginability, and divergent thinking. This is the most directly creativity-relevant study in this line.

**Paper 4: Wiseman, Wiles & Watt (2022)**
- *PeerJ*, "Experiencing the impossible and creativity: a targeted literature review"
- Key finding: Across 6 domains including science fiction/fantasy reading, correlational and experimental evidence consistently supports that experiencing the "impossible" (which includes fiction) promotes creative thinking through schema disruption. Science fiction readers show greater openness and divergent thinking. Middle school students who watched sci-fi subsequently produced more creative products.

#### Contradictory/Limiting Evidence
- **Replication failures**: Four replication experiments of Kidd & Castano (2013) with N=1,006 found null results for the experimental effect, while replicating the correlational finding (habitual fiction readers perform better on ToM tasks). The acute experimental effect may be fragile.
- Most evidence is about empathy/Theory of Mind, not divergent thinking specifically
- The fiction-creativity link is largely indirect: fiction -> reduced cognitive closure -> openness -> creativity (multi-step mediation)
- Selection effects: people who read fiction may already be more open/creative
- The 2022 replication/extension study's results on divergent thinking specifically are not yet widely cited
- "Literary fiction" vs. "popular fiction" distinction is subjective and difficult to operationalize

#### Mechanism Summary
Fiction reading operates as a cognitive and emotional simulation of social experience (Mar & Oatley, 2008). Literary fiction requires active imaginative engagement to construct characters' inner mental states, which exercises perspective-taking and theory of mind. This process reduces need for cognitive closure, increases openness to new experience, and promotes cognitive flexibility. Science fiction/fantasy specifically disrupts existing schemas by presenting impossible scenarios, forcing conceptual recombination. Transportation into narrative (emotional engagement with the story) is a key moderator -- only transported readers show the effect.

#### Scores
| Axis | Score | Justification |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Evidence Reliability | **3** | Kidd & Castano (2013) in Science is landmark but has replication issues; Djikic/Oatley provide supporting experiments; the fiction-to-creativity path is indirect (through ToM, openness, closure); the 2022 extension to divergent thinking is promising but limited |
| Effect Size | **2** | Effects on ToM are small-to-medium; replication found null acute effects; the chain from fiction to creativity is indirect with likely attenuation at each step; schema disruption effects (sci-fi) are suggestive but not quantified with Cohen's d |
| Ease of Adoption | **5** | Read books; free from library; can start immediately; highly enjoyable for most people; zero barriers |
